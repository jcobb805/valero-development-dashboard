Why Option A Wins on Efficiency

  • Highest unlevered yield: 3.6% vs 2.1% (duplex) and 2.4% (3-story). This is the single most important metric for a leveraged hold.
  • Lowest capital requirement: $86K equity vs $124-125K. More capital efficient; can diversify elsewhere.
  • Strongest direct comp: 130 Valero is a 2020-built 3/2 two-story modern on the same block currently renting at $3,285/mo — this is as close to signed lease data as you can get without actually signing a lease.
  • By-right zoning: 4-6 week plan review, no HDRC, no rezone. Fastest path to rent-producing asset.
  • Simplest operational profile: One tenant, one lease, one turnover cycle — vs two units in a duplex.
  • Best cross-over timeline: ATCF crosses positive earliest (~Yr 4-5) because of highest unlevered yield.

When Option C (3-Story) Beats Option A

Option C produces more absolute wealth ($1.17M vs $920K at Yr 30) because the bigger building appreciates in bigger dollars. But it does so at a lower rate of return (7.4% vs 8.6% 10-yr IRR).

Choose Option C if you:

  • Have excess capital to deploy and can absorb more negative cash flow in early years
  • Prefer a differentiated/premium product (3-story townhome-style stands out in Tobin Hill)
  • Want a long-duration (20-30 year) wealth-building vehicle at max scale on the lot
  • Plan to own-occupy someday (walkable downtown, covered garage, modern finishes)

Choose Option A if you want the highest % return per dollar invested and the cleanest financing path.

Option B (Duplex) — Avoid Under Current Conditions

Duplex fails on all three axes for a hold investor: (1) lowest unlevered yield (2.1%) — essentially the worst version of negative leverage, (2) requires rezoning (6-12 months + denial risk + $45-55K in delay costs), (3) weakest appreciation driver (duplex sale comps in Tobin Hill are thin; SFR comps are abundant). Revisit in 2027-2028 if San Antonio passes missing-middle reform OR if rates fall below 5%.

30-Year Hold Pro Forma — Cash Flow Trajectory

At 80% debt and 7% rates, Year-1 cash flow is negative. But rents grow at 3%/yr while debt service stays fixed, so pre-tax cash flow crosses to positive around Year 4-5 (SFR), longer for duplex/3-story. After-tax cash flow (with depreciation shield) crosses positive even earlier.

Equity Build-Up Over Time

Equity = property value − loan balance. Property appreciates at 3%/yr; loan amortizes down. By Year 30, loan is paid off and equity equals full property value.

Wealth Created (Cumulative)

Cumulative total return = all ATCF + final equity value − initial equity. This is the true "how much richer am I" metric.

The Honest Truth

New-construction rental at 80% debt in 2026 Texas does not cash-flow positively in Year 1. That's structural across the entire market — not a bug in this specific deal. The investors making money right now are those who:

  1. Already own the land (no land basis) — ✓ you
  2. Accept 2-3 years of negative pre-tax CF as the price of admission
  3. Plan to refinance when rates drop to 5-5.5% (likely 2027-2028)
  4. Value the depreciation tax shield (currently ~$6.9K/yr after-tax benefit)
  5. Have long time horizons (15+ years) to let appreciation and paydown work

If ALL of those apply to you — Option A pencils as an ~9-10% long-term levered return on a tangible asset. That's competitive with the S&P 500's historical average, with tangible collateral, inflation protection, and tax benefits not available in stocks. The fact that Year-1 ATCF is −$5K is not disqualifying; it's the entry ticket.

If any of them don't apply (e.g., you need cash flow today, won't hold 15+ years, can't stomach refinancing risk) — sell the lot and put the proceeds in a higher-yield passive vehicle. Don't build.
